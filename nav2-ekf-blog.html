What Does "Navigation" Really Mean?

Navigation in robotics is the problem of moving an autonomous agent from a given start state to a desired goal state while respecting the physics of the robot, the geometry of its environment, and — in real deployments — uncertainty in sensing and actuation. It decomposes into three interleaved sub-problems:

🗺️ Mapping

Building or using a representation of the environment — occupied vs. free space, obstacle geometry, or semantic labels.

📍 Localization

Estimating the robot's own pose within the map, typically via sensor fusion (IMU, LIDAR, cameras) and Bayesian filtering.

🧭 Planning & Control

Computing a collision-free trajectory through free space and tracking it with the robot's actuators under kinematic/dynamic constraints.

This blog focuses on the third pillar — planning and the algorithms that enable it. We treat both local and global approaches, derive them mathematically, and animate them on structured examples.

Configuration Space: The Foundational Abstraction

Section 1 — Core Theory

The Workspace vs. the Configuration Space

The workspace \(\mathcal{W} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d\) (with \(d=2\) or \(3\)) is the physical space in which the robot and obstacles exist. The robot body occupies a set \(\mathcal{A}(\mathbf{q}) \subset \mathcal{W}\) that depends on the robot's current configuration \(\mathbf{q}\). Obstacles occupy \(\mathcal{O} \subset \mathcal{W}\).

Collision Condition (Workspace) $$\text{Collision if and only if} \quad \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{q}) \cap \mathcal{O} \neq \emptyset$$

The configuration space \(\mathcal{C}\) is the space of all possible configurations \(\mathbf{q}\). For a planar rigid body, \(\mathbf{q} = (x, y, \theta) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \times S^1\). For a robot arm with \(n\) joints, \(\mathbf{q} = (\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n) \in \mathbb{T}^n\).

C-Space Obstacle and Free Space $$\mathcal{C}_{\text{obs}} = \{ \mathbf{q} \in \mathcal{C} \mid \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{q}) \cap \mathcal{O} \neq \emptyset \}$$ $$\mathcal{C}_{\text{free}} = \mathcal{C} \setminus \mathcal{C}_{\text{obs}}$$
Key insight: The planning problem in workspace (avoiding 3D geometry) transforms into a path problem in \(\mathcal{C}_{\text{free}}\): find a continuous path \(\gamma : [0,1] \to \mathcal{C}_{\text{free}}\) such that \(\gamma(0) = \mathbf{q}_0\) and \(\gamma(1) = \mathbf{q}_f\).

Computing C-Space Obstacles (2D Robot Example)

For a convex polygonal robot \(\mathcal{A}\) and a convex obstacle \(\mathcal{O}\) in the plane, the C-space obstacle is the Minkowski difference:

Minkowski Difference (point robot after inflation) $$\mathcal{C}_{\text{obs}} = \mathcal{O} \oplus (-\mathcal{A}) = \{ \mathbf{p} - \mathbf{a} \mid \mathbf{p} \in \mathcal{O},\; \mathbf{a} \in \mathcal{A} \}$$

Geometrically, this inflates each obstacle by the robot's footprint. A point robot navigating in the inflated space is exactly equivalent to the full robot navigating in the original space. This is why mobile-robot planners typically work with an "inflated costmap."

Local Planning: Artificial Potential Fields

Section 2 — Local Methods

The Core Idea

Artificial potential fields (APF), introduced by Khatib (1986), treat the robot as a particle in an energy landscape. The goal exerts an attractive force; obstacles exert repulsive forces. The robot follows the negative gradient — steepest descent — of the total potential.

Total Potential $$U(\mathbf{q}) = U_{\text{att}}(\mathbf{q}) + U_{\text{rep}}(\mathbf{q})$$ $$\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{q}) = -\nabla_{\mathbf{q}}\, U(\mathbf{q})$$

Attractive Potential — Full Derivation

Let \(\mathbf{q}_g\) be the goal configuration. Define the distance \(\rho_g(\mathbf{q}) = \|\mathbf{q} - \mathbf{q}_g\|\). A conic well is simple but has a discontinuous gradient at the origin; a quadratic is smooth but grows unboundedly. The standard fix is the conic-quadratic blend:

Conic-Quadratic Attractive Potential $$U_{\text{att}}(\mathbf{q}) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{1}{2}\, k_{\text{att}}\, \rho_g^2(\mathbf{q}) & \rho_g \leq d^* \\ d^*\, k_{\text{att}}\, \rho_g(\mathbf{q}) - \dfrac{1}{2}\, k_{\text{att}}\, (d^*)^2 & \rho_g > d^* \end{cases}$$

The resulting attractive force is:

Attractive Force $$\mathbf{F}_{\text{att}}(\mathbf{q}) = -\nabla U_{\text{att}} = \begin{cases} -k_{\text{att}}\,(\mathbf{q} - \mathbf{q}_g) & \rho_g \leq d^* \\ -d^*\, k_{\text{att}}\, \dfrac{\mathbf{q}-\mathbf{q}_g}{\rho_g} & \rho_g > d^* \end{cases}$$

Repulsive Potential — Full Derivation

For each obstacle, let \(\rho(\mathbf{q})\) be the distance from configuration \(\mathbf{q}\) to the nearest obstacle boundary. Let \(Q^*\) be the influence radius of the obstacle.

Repulsive Potential (Khatib 1986) $$U_{\text{rep}}(\mathbf{q}) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{1}{2}\, k_{\text{rep}} \left(\dfrac{1}{\rho(\mathbf{q})} - \dfrac{1}{Q^*}\right)^2 & \rho \leq Q^* \\ 0 & \rho > Q^* \end{cases}$$

Taking the gradient:

Repulsive Force $$\mathbf{F}_{\text{rep}}(\mathbf{q}) = \begin{cases} k_{\text{rep}}\left(\dfrac{1}{\rho} - \dfrac{1}{Q^*}\right)\dfrac{1}{\rho^2}\,\nabla\rho & \rho \leq Q^* \\ \mathbf{0} & \rho > Q^* \end{cases}$$

Note that \(\nabla \rho\) points away from the nearest obstacle surface, so \(\mathbf{F}_{\text{rep}}\) pushes the robot away from obstacles. As \(\rho \to 0\), \(\mathbf{F}_{\text{rep}} \to \infty\) — an infinite barrier.

The Local-Minima Problem

A configuration \(\mathbf{q}^*\) is a local minimum of \(U\) if \(\nabla U(\mathbf{q}^*) = \mathbf{0}\) but \(\mathbf{q}^* \neq \mathbf{q}_g\). At such a point, attractive and repulsive forces exactly cancel — the robot is stuck.

$$\nabla U_{\text{att}}(\mathbf{q}^*) + \nabla U_{\text{rep}}(\mathbf{q}^*) = \mathbf{0} \quad \text{(spurious equilibrium)}$$
Fundamental limitation: Potential field methods are incomplete. U-shaped obstacles facing the goal direction reliably produce local minima. The fix is either to use navigation functions (provably local-minima-free on sphere-worlds) or to switch to a global planner.

Global Planning: Graph Search — Dijkstra and A*

Section 3 — Optimal Graph Search

Discretisation: From \(\mathcal{C}_{\text{free}}\) to a Graph

The continuous planning problem is intractable in general. A standard approach is to discretise \(\mathcal{C}_{\text{free}}\) into a graph \(G = (V, E)\) where:

  • Each vertex \(v \in V\) is a collision-free configuration sample.
  • Each edge \((u,v) \in E\) with weight \(w(u,v) \geq 0\) represents a collision-free local motion whose cost is the arc length (or travel time).

Path planning reduces to finding the shortest path from the start vertex \(s\) to the goal vertex \(g\) in \(G\).

Dijkstra's Algorithm — Full Derivation

Dijkstra's algorithm (1959) solves the single-source shortest-path problem on graphs with non-negative edge weights. It maintains a cost-to-come estimate \(g(v)\) for each vertex, initialised as:

Initialisation $$g(s) = 0, \qquad g(v) = +\infty \quad \forall\, v \neq s$$

The algorithm uses a min-priority queue \(\text{OPEN}\) ordered by \(g\). At each step:

  1. Extract \(u = \arg\min_{v \in \text{OPEN}} g(v)\).
  2. For each neighbour \(n\) of \(u\): if \(g(u) + w(u,n) < g(n)\), relax the edge: $$g(n) \leftarrow g(u) + w(u,n), \qquad \text{parent}(n) \leftarrow u$$
  3. Move \(u\) to \(\text{CLOSED}\).
Relaxation Condition (Bellman's Principle) $$g^*(v) = \min_{u \in \text{pred}(v)} \left[ g^*(u) + w(u,v) \right]$$

Correctness proof sketch: When a vertex \(u\) is extracted from OPEN, \(g(u)\) is already optimal. This follows by induction: any other path to \(u\) must pass through a vertex still in OPEN, which by the min-heap property has cost \(\geq g(u)\), and edge weights are non-negative so no shortcut exists.

Complexity $$T = O\bigl((|V| + |E|)\log|V|\bigr) \quad \text{with binary heap}$$

A* Algorithm — Full Derivation

A* (Hart, Nilsson, Raphael 1968) augments Dijkstra with a heuristic \(h : V \to [0,\infty)\) that estimates the cost-to-go from each vertex to the goal. The priority key becomes:

A* Priority (Evaluation Function) $$f(v) = g(v) + h(v)$$

where \(g(v)\) is the exact cost-to-come and \(h(v)\) is the heuristic estimate of cost-to-go.

Admissibility Condition

Let \(h^*(v)\) denote the true optimal cost-to-go. The heuristic is admissible (never over-estimates) if:

$$h(v) \leq h^*(v) \quad \forall\, v \in V$$

Theorem (A* optimality): If \(h\) is admissible, A* returns an optimal path.

Proof sketch: Suppose A* returns a suboptimal path to \(g\) with cost \(C' > C^*\). Then there exists a node \(n\) on the optimal path still in OPEN. We have:

$$f(n) = g(n) + h(n) \leq g^*(n) + h^*(n) = C^* < C'$$

So \(n\) would be expanded before \(g\) — contradiction. \(\square\)

Consistency (Monotonicity)

A stronger condition is consistency: for every edge \((u,v)\),

$$h(u) \leq w(u,v) + h(v)$$

Consistency implies admissibility and also means \(f\)-values are non-decreasing along any path A* expands, so each vertex is expanded at most once.

Dijkstra vs A* — The Information-Theoretic View

Dijkstra knows only where the robot has been (\(g\)). A* knows where it has been and roughly how far it still needs to go (\(h\)). The more informative the heuristic (tighter lower bound), the fewer states A* needs to expand. In the limit, a perfect heuristic \(h = h^*\) causes A* to expand only the nodes on the optimal path.

Dijkstra: "Expand the node I know is cheapest to reach."
A*: "Expand the node I estimate gives the cheapest complete path."

Sampling-Based Planning: PRM and RRT

Section 5 — Probabilistic Methods

Why Grid Search Breaks Down

Grid-based search suffers from the curse of dimensionality. For a \(d\)-dimensional configuration space with resolution \(\varepsilon\), the number of grid cells scales as \(O(\varepsilon^{-d})\). A 6-DOF robot arm with \(\varepsilon = 0.01\) rad requires \(\sim 10^{12}\) cells — computationally intractable.

Probabilistic Roadmap Method (PRM)

PRM (Kavraki et al., 1996) constructs a roadmap by sampling random configurations in \(\mathcal{C}_{\text{free}}\) and connecting nearby samples with a local planner.

PRM Algorithm $$\textbf{Learning phase:}$$ $$\text{For } i = 1,\ldots,N: \quad \mathbf{q}_i \sim \text{Uniform}(\mathcal{C}), \quad \text{if } \mathbf{q}_i \in \mathcal{C}_{\text{free}} \Rightarrow V \leftarrow V \cup \{\mathbf{q}_i\}$$ $$\text{For each } \mathbf{q} \in V: \text{ connect to } k\text{-nearest } \mathbf{q}' \in V \text{ if local path } \mathbf{q}\to\mathbf{q}' \subset \mathcal{C}_{\text{free}}$$ $$\textbf{Query phase:} \quad \text{Connect } \mathbf{q}_0, \mathbf{q}_f \text{ to roadmap, run Dijkstra/A*}$$

Asymptotic optimality: PRM* (Karaman & Frazzoli, 2011) modifies the connection radius to \(r = \gamma\left(\frac{\log N}{N}\right)^{1/d}\) (for a problem-dependent \(\gamma\)), guaranteeing that the solution cost converges to optimal as \(N \to \infty\).

Rapidly-Exploring Random Trees (RRT)

RRT (LaValle, 1998) grows a tree from the start configuration by iteratively sampling a random state and extending the tree toward it.

RRT Extension Step $$\mathbf{q}_{\text{rand}} \sim \text{Uniform}(\mathcal{C})$$ $$\mathbf{q}_{\text{near}} = \arg\min_{\mathbf{q} \in T} \|\mathbf{q} - \mathbf{q}_{\text{rand}}\|$$ $$\mathbf{q}_{\text{new}} = \mathbf{q}_{\text{near}} + \delta \cdot \frac{\mathbf{q}_{\text{rand}} - \mathbf{q}_{\text{near}}}{\|\mathbf{q}_{\text{rand}} - \mathbf{q}_{\text{near}}\|}$$ $$\text{If edge } (\mathbf{q}_{\text{near}}, \mathbf{q}_{\text{new}}) \subset \mathcal{C}_{\text{free}}: \quad T \leftarrow T \cup \{(\mathbf{q}_{\text{near}}, \mathbf{q}_{\text{new}})\}$$

The key geometric property of RRT is Voronoi bias: at each step, the probability that a node is selected for extension equals the volume of its Voronoi region relative to all other nodes. Nodes with large Voronoi regions (at the frontier of the explored space) are selected most often, so the tree expands rapidly into unexplored territory.

RRT* (asymptotically optimal): After each extension, RRT* rewires the tree by checking if newly found vertices can be connected to other near vertices through cheaper paths. This ensures the solution cost converges to optimal as samples → ∞.

PRM — Multi-Query

Build the roadmap once, answer many queries. Best when the environment is static and many robot tasks must be planned.

Complexity: \(O(N \log N)\) construction, \(O(|V|+|E|)\log|V|)\) per query.

RRT — Single-Query

Grow a tree on demand toward the goal. Best for a single query or dynamic environments where the roadmap would be stale.

Complexity: \(O(N \log N)\) per query with \(k\)-d tree nearest-neighbour.

Mobile Robot Kinematics and Nonholonomic Constraints

Section 6 — Mobile Robot Models

The Unicycle Model

The unicycle (or kinematic car without steering limits) is the canonical mobile robot model. It captures the rolling-without-slipping assumption: the wheel's velocity vector always points along the robot heading \(\theta\).

Unicycle Kinematic Model $$\dot{x} = v\cos\theta, \qquad \dot{y} = v\sin\theta, \qquad \dot{\theta} = \omega$$

Eliminating \(v\) by differentiating \(\dot{x}/\cos\theta = \dot{y}/\sin\theta\) yields the nonholonomic constraint:

Nonholonomic (Pfaffian) Constraint $$\dot{x}\sin\theta - \dot{y}\cos\theta = 0$$ $$\Leftrightarrow \quad \begin{bmatrix} \sin\theta & -\cos\theta & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}\dot{x}\\\dot{y}\\\dot{\theta}\end{bmatrix} = 0$$

This is a velocity constraint (non-integrable), not a position constraint. The robot can still reach any \((x,y,\theta)\) in \(\mathbb{R}^2 \times S^1\) — but not in an arbitrary direction at a given instant. This is what makes mobile robot planning fundamentally harder than holonomic planning.

Differential Drive Robot

Most wheeled indoor robots use differential drive: two independently controlled wheels of radius \(r_w\) on an axle of half-width \(l_w\). Let \(\omega_l, \omega_r\) be left/right wheel angular velocities.

Forward Kinematics (Differential Drive) $$v = \frac{r_w}{2}(\omega_r + \omega_l), \qquad \omega = \frac{r_w}{2 l_w}(\omega_r - \omega_l)$$

The inverse maps body velocities back to wheel rates:

Inverse Kinematics $$\omega_r = \frac{v + l_w \omega}{r_w}, \qquad \omega_l = \frac{v - l_w \omega}{r_w}$$

Special cases: \(\omega_l = \omega_r\) → straight line; \(\omega_l = -\omega_r\) → rotate in place (radius \(= 0\)); general case → circular arc of radius \(R = l_w(\omega_r+\omega_l)/(\omega_r-\omega_l)\).

Bicycle / Front-Wheel Steering Model (Ackermann)

For cars and many outdoor AGVs, the front wheels steer while the rear wheels drive. With wheelbase \(L\) and steering angle \(\xi\):

Kinematic Bicycle Model $$\dot{x} = v\cos\theta, \quad \dot{y} = v\sin\theta, \quad \dot{\theta} = \frac{v}{L}\tan\xi$$

Unlike differential drive, this robot cannot rotate in place. The minimum turning radius is \(R_{\min} = L/\tan\xi_{\max}\), which must be respected in path planning (Dubins paths, Reeds-Shepp curves).

Differential Flatness — Planning in Output Space

A system is differentially flat if there exists a flat output \(\mathbf{z}(t)\) such that all states and inputs can be expressed algebraically in terms of \(\mathbf{z}\) and a finite number of its time derivatives — without integrating the dynamics.

Flat Output for the Unicycle $$\mathbf{z} = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} \quad \Rightarrow \quad v = \pm\sqrt{\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2}, \quad \theta = \text{atan2}(\dot{y}, \dot{x}), \quad \omega = \frac{\ddot{y}\dot{x} - \ddot{x}\dot{y}}{\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2}$$

This is a powerful result: design \(x(t), y(t)\) as any smooth parametric curve (e.g., cubic splines, Bézier), then recover} the robot's heading and angular velocity analytically. No integration needed.

Cubic Spline Trajectory (Example) $$x(t) = a_0 + a_1 t + a_2 t^2 + a_3 t^3$$ $$y(t) = b_0 + b_1 t + b_2 t^2 + b_3 t^3$$ $$\text{Boundary conditions: } x(0),\dot{x}(0),x(T),\dot{x}(T) \text{ fix } a_0,\ldots,a_3 \text{ uniquely}$$
Why flatness matters for planners: Instead of solving a constrained ODE boundary-value problem, path planning reduces to unconstrained curve design in \((x,y)\)-space. The kinematic constraints are satisfied automatically by the reconstruction formulae.
