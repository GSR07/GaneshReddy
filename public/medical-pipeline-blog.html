The Pipeline Is the Product: ML for Medical Imaging

Dataset curation, quality checks, bias analysis, and failure-case review — the unglamorous machinery around the model that decides whether medical ML is real or fiction.

1. The Unglamorous 80%

After building an X-ray classifier, I rebuilt the surrounding machinery properly: a supervised learning pipeline for medical imaging where the model is one component among many — and rarely the one that fails. This post is about the other components: curation, quality checks, bias analysis, and failure-case review.

2. Dataset Curation

Public medical datasets are stitched together from multiple hospitals, scanners, and labelling processes. Before any training:

  • Deduplication — near-duplicate images (same study, re-exported) silently leak between train and test splits.
  • Label provenance — some labels come from radiologist reads, others from NLP over reports. These are not the same quality, and mixing them unweighted degrades both.
  • Patient-level splitting — the single most common invalidating mistake in published medical ML; all images of a patient go to exactly one split.

3. Quality Checks and Bias Analysis

Two questions, asked before training: can the model cheat? and who is missing?

Cheating: scanner metadata burned into image corners, hospital-specific positioning markers, and even text tokens correlate with the label when one hospital contributed most of the positive cases. A model that reads the corner annotation gets a great test score and zero clinical value. Cropping and per-source score audits catch this.

Coverage: stratifying performance by source, sex, and age bands exposes cohorts where the model quietly underperforms — a per-cohort recall table communicates more than any single ROC curve.

4. Failure-Case Analysis

Every evaluation run ends with the same ritual: pull the highest-confidence wrong predictions and look at them, one by one. The categories that emerge — mislabelled ground truth, genuinely ambiguous cases, image-quality failures, and true model errors — each demand a different fix, and none of them is "train longer". Aggregate metrics tell you that the model is imperfect; failure review tells you why, and whether the fix belongs in the data, the labels, or the model.

5. Takeaways

  • The pipeline is the product; the model is a replaceable part inside it.
  • Most "model" failures are data failures wearing a disguise.
  • Bias analysis is not a compliance step — it is where the real generalisation problems show up first.
