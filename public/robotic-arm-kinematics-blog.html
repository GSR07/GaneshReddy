5-Actuator Open Manipulator — Forward & Inverse Kinematics

A ROS-controlled articulated arm mounted on an AMR for warehouse pick-and-place. DH parameters, closed-form FK, and a geometric closed-form IK solution — and why 5 actuators only make 4 degrees of freedom.

1. Hardware & Configuration

This arm rides on an Autonomous Mobile Robot (AMR) and does pick-and-place in a warehouse/logistics setting. It uses 5 Dynamixel XM430 actuators — but kinematically it is a 4-DoF articulated positioning arm (base yaw + three coplanar revolute joints) plus a 1-DoF parallel-jaw gripper. The gripper opens and closes the end-effector; it does not add a positioning degree of freedom. Everything below is derived for the 4-DoF positioning chain.

The arm is a serial anthropomorphic (articulated) manipulator: the first joint sits on the fixed base and the chain terminates in a free end-effector. Joint 1 yaws the whole arm about the vertical; joints 2–4 then act as a planar linkage in a single plane.

2. Denavit–Hartenberg (DH) Parameters

DH frame assignment. After the α₁ = 90° twist, the z-axes of joints 2–4 point out of the page (⊙) — the arm becomes a planar linkage.

Frames follow the standard DH convention. A geometric offset angle \(\theta_0\) exists between joints 2 and 3 because the physical link is bent; it is measured from the flange geometry and folded into the joint variables:

\[ \theta_0 = \tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{a}{b}\right) \]

1\(\theta_1\)9000.080
2\(\theta_2 - \theta_0\)00.1750

Symbols: \(\theta_i\) = joint angle (variable), \(\alpha_i\) = link twist, \(a_i\) = link length, \(d_i\) = joint offset.

Key structural insight: the \(\alpha_1 = 90°\) twist tips the arm into a vertical plane after the base yaw. Since joints 2–4 all have \(\alpha = 0\), the arm reduces to a planar 3-link problem in that plane — which is exactly what makes the inverse kinematics solvable in closed form.

3. Forward Kinematics

Each link's homogeneous transform from frame \(i{-}1\) to frame \(i\):

\[ T_i^{\,i-1} = \begin{bmatrix} c\theta_i & -s\theta_i c\alpha_i & s\theta_i s\alpha_i & a_i c\theta_i \\ s\theta_i & c\theta_i c\alpha_i & -c\theta_i s\alpha_i & a_i s\theta_i \\ 0 & s\alpha_i & c\alpha_i & d_i \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

where \(c = \cos\), \(s = \sin\).

The end-effector pose is the ordered product of all four link transforms:

\[ T_4^{\,0} = T_1^{\,0}\, T_2^{\,1}\, T_3^{\,2}\, T_4^{\,3} = \begin{bmatrix} n_x & o_x & a_x & p_x \\ n_y & o_y & a_y & p_y \\ n_z & o_z & a_z & p_z \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \]
  • \(p = (p_x, p_y, p_z)\) — end-effector position.
  • \(n\), \(o\), \(a\) — orthonormal columns defining end-effector orientation (normal, orientation, approach axes).

FK in one line: given \((\theta_1, \theta_2, \theta_3, \theta_4)\), evaluate the product to obtain the full \(4\times 4\) pose.

4. Inverse Kinematics (Closed-Form, Geometric)

The IK is solved in two stages: the base yaw is decoupled from the planar sub-problem, and the remaining three joints are solved geometrically in the \(r\)–\(z\) plane.

Step 1 — Base yaw \(\theta_1\)

Motion of the arm across the \(x\)–\(y\) plane depends only on joint 1:

\[ \theta_1 = \operatorname{atan2}(p_y,\, p_x), \qquad p_r = \sqrt{p_x^2 + p_y^2} \]

\(\theta_1\) is the base rotation, ranging \(-180° \dots 180°\). The \(x\) and \(y\) coordinates are merged into a single radial axis \(r\).

Steps 2–5 — Planar sub-problem in the \(r\)–\(z\) plane

Project the target into the vertical plane and subtract the base offset:

\[ r_3 = p_r, \qquad z_3 = p_z - d_1 \]

Using the desired approach angle \(\phi = \theta_2 + \theta_3 + \theta_4\), back off the last link to find the wrist point:

\[ r_2 = r_3 - a_4\cos\phi, \qquad z_2 = z_3 - a_4\sin\phi \]

Elbow angle \(\theta_3\) via the law of cosines (the \(\pm\) selects elbow-up or elbow-down):

\[ \cos\theta_3 = \frac{r_2^2 + z_2^2 - (a_2^2 + a_3^2)}{2\,a_2\,a_3}, \qquad \theta_3 = \pm\cos^{-1}(\cdot) \]

Shoulder angle \(\theta_2\):

\[ \cos\theta_2 = \frac{(a_2 + a_3\cos\theta_3)\,r_2 + (a_3\sin\theta_3)\,z_2}{r_2^2 + z_2^2} \] \[ \sin\theta_2 = \frac{(a_2 + a_3\cos\theta_3)\,z_2 - (a_3\sin\theta_3)\,r_2}{r_2^2 + z_2^2} \] \[ \theta_2 = \operatorname{atan2}(\sin\theta_2,\, \cos\theta_2) \]

Wrist angle \(\theta_4\):

\[ \theta_4 = \phi - (\theta_2 + \theta_3) \]

Link constants: \(a_2 = 0.175\), \(a_3 = 0.200\), \(a_4 = 0.135\), \(d_1 = 0.080\) (metres).
Joint limits: \(\theta_2, \theta_3, \theta_4 \in [-90°,\, 90°]\).

Elbow-up vs elbow-down

The \(\pm\) in the \(\cos^{-1}\) yields two valid joint sets that reach the same end-effector pose. The correct branch is chosen by joint limits and self-collision avoidance.

5. Implementation Notes

  • atan2, not atan: every angle uses the two-argument arctangent to keep the correct quadrant — a single-argument \(\tan^{-1}\) silently collapses \(\theta\) into \(\pm 90°\) and breaks reachability across quadrants.
  • Reachability check: before solving, verify \(\lvert r_2^2 + z_2^2 - a_2^2 - a_3^2 \rvert \le 2 a_2 a_3\); if \(\lvert\cos\theta_3\rvert > 1\) the target is outside the workspace.
  • Stack: kinematics run in the ROS control loop; the arm is commanded through the Dynamixel SDK over U2D2, with the AMR handling navigation to the pick/place pose.

Part of an Autonomous Mobile Robot (AMR) project for warehouse logistics — differential-drive base with LiDAR SLAM, A* path planning, EKF sensor fusion, and this articulated arm for pick-and-place.
