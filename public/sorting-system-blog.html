Grading Parts in Real Time: Classical CV That Ships

How an HSV + contour pipeline hit 90% accuracy at 5–10 items/min on a student budget — and why most of the accuracy was won with lighting, not algorithms.

KEY CONCEPTS: HSV Segmentation

1. The Problem

My bachelor's thesis: an automated grading and sorting system — parts arrive continuously, a camera watches them, software decides the grade, and mechanical actuators route each part to its bin. Four people, one semester, and a hard requirement that the whole loop runs in real time at 5–10 items per minute. I led the computer vision module and owned its integration with the SolidWorks-designed actuation hardware.

2. Pipeline Architecture

The classical CV pipeline still wins in constrained industrial settings — it is fast, explainable, and tunable on-site without retraining anything. Ours had five stages:

  1. Capture — fixed overhead camera, controlled lighting rig to kill shadows and specular highlights before they reach software.
  2. Preprocess — Gaussian blur to suppress sensor noise, then conversion to HSV, where object colour separates from illumination intensity.
  3. Segment — colour thresholding plus morphological opening/closing to produce a clean binary mask per part.
  4. Classify — contour features (area, aspect ratio, solidity) and colour statistics grade each part against calibrated reference ranges.
  5. Actuate — the grade is sent to the sorting mechanism, which diverts the part to the matching bin.

3. Why HSV and Contours

RGB thresholds break the moment lighting shifts. In HSV, hue is largely invariant to brightness, so a part's colour signature survives lamp warm-up and daylight changes. From the binary mask, cv2.findContours gives each part's outline, and a handful of cheap geometric descriptors — contour area, bounding-box aspect ratio, convexity defects — turned out to separate our part grades far more reliably than any single feature.

The lesson that stuck: 90% of classification accuracy was won in the physical world — lighting, background, camera angle — not in the code. We reached 90% classification accuracy, and nearly every residual error traced back to part presentation, not the algorithm.

4. Integrating with the Mechanism

Vision that cannot act is a screensaver. The timing contract mattered: a part must be classified before it reaches the diverter, so the pipeline's worst-case latency — not its average — set the conveyor speed. We profiled every stage, pinned the slow tail (contour analysis on noisy masks), and fixed it upstream with better morphology rather than downstream with faster code.

The mechanical side — actuator geometry and bin layout designed in SolidWorks by my teammates — was co-designed with the vision timing budget, which is the part of the project that taught me the most about engineering as a team sport.

5. Takeaways

  • Classical CV is not legacy CV — for controlled scenes it beats deep learning on latency, hardware cost, and debuggability.
  • Fix problems as far upstream as possible: a €10 lamp beat hours of algorithm tuning.
  • Real-time means worst-case, not average-case.
