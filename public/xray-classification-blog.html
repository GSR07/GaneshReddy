Chest X-ray Classification with 5,000 Images

Fine-tuning ResNet and VGG for pneumonia and COVID-19 detection — and why the evaluation design mattered more than the architecture choice.

1. The Task

Classify chest X-rays into pneumonia, COVID-19, and normal classes — with roughly 5,000 labelled images. That number is the whole story: it is far too few to train a modern CNN from scratch, and exactly enough to fine-tune one. This post covers the transfer-learning setup, what actually moved the accuracy, and the evaluation traps specific to medical imaging.

2. Transfer Learning Setup

Both backbones I compared — ResNet and VGG — were initialised from ImageNet weights. The recipe:

  1. Replace the final fully-connected layer with a fresh head sized to the number of classes.
  2. Train the head first with the backbone frozen — the pretrained features are already decent edge/texture detectors.
  3. Unfreeze the last convolutional stages and fine-tune everything at a much lower learning rate.

Training minimises the standard cross-entropy over classes:

\[ \mathcal{L} = -\sum_{c=1}^{C} y_c \log \hat{p}_c \]

with class weights folded in, because the dataset — like nearly every medical dataset — was imbalanced across conditions.

3. What Actually Helped

  • Class weighting over naive oversampling — duplicating rare-class X-rays mostly taught the network to memorise them.
  • Conservative augmentation. Small rotations and translations help; horizontal flips are dangerous — anatomy is not symmetric (the heart has a side), and aggressive augmentation quietly destroys diagnostic signal.
  • Lower learning rates for deeper unfreezing. Fine-tuning with the head's learning rate wipes out the pretrained features you came for.

4. Evaluating Honestly

The headline number was 85% accuracy, but accuracy alone is nearly meaningless in a medical setting. Per-class recall matters more: a pneumonia classifier that misses pneumonia is worse than useless regardless of its accuracy. The confusion matrix, per-class precision/recall, and a look at the actual misclassified films were the real evaluation — several "errors" turned out to be genuinely ambiguous cases that clinicians also flag as borderline.

Equally important: patient-level splits. If images from the same patient land in both train and test sets, the network can recognise the patient (or the scanner) instead of the disease, and your test score is fiction.

5. Takeaways

  • With thousands (not millions) of images, transfer learning is not one option — it is the only option.
  • Augmentation policy is a domain decision, not a hyperparameter sweep.
  • In medical ML, the evaluation design is harder — and more important — than the model.
